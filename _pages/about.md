Our lab is interested in understanding how people navigate the social world. We ask questions about:
- How people make social decisions
- How they interpret others' social cues
- How attitudes, goals, and motivations shape social behaviour
- How stable people's behaviour is across their interactions

Our philosophy is that it is impossible to understand real social behaviour and how it works without studying real social behaviour. Therefore, one of our primary research methods involves asking people to interact with one another in unscripted, face-to-face interactions. We often follow up such studies with experimental laboratory work and questionnaire-based designs to help confirm our ideas.


Current Projects
======

Social Signals in Naturalistic Conversations
------

- *Political Communication:* In collaboration with the [Body-Politics Lab @ Western](https://www.bodypoliticslab.amandafriesen.com/) we are investigating how people communicate political and non-political attitudes, emotions, personality indicators, and other social cues during naturalistic interactions using conversation prompts. This study uses a round-robin design to investigate how people change their behaviour both within interactions with a single person as well as across interaction partners. We will then use people's behaviour patterns to predict their ratings of their interactions and social partners.

- *Signals of Empathy:* In collaboration with the [Laboratory on Social and Affective Neuroscience @ Georgetown](https://abigailmarsh.com/) we are looking at how traits such as extremely high levels of empathy for others shape interaction quality. We are examining how reciprocity in the exchange of social cues might help put others at ease and make conversations run more smoothly.


Attitudes, Emotions, and Appraisals
------

- *Cognitive Dissonance Revisited:* In this project we are revisiting the effects that cognitive dissonance might have on people's moods and attitudes as a pre-cursor to looking at how this might shape social behaviour. Using a within-subjects design, we ask participants to write persuasive statements about things that they do and do not believe in order to understand about how their emotions and attitudes change when they do and do not believe the things they are saying. Mikayla Colthirst-Reid is the PhD student spearheading this work.

- *Status Cues and Narcissism:* Here we are examining how people with varying levels of trait narcissism appraise and attend to social cues that are both associated with status attainment and status loss. This work is currently in an experimental phase, in which we link self-report data with experimental data. In the next phase of the project, we will look at the nature and quality of interactions between people with varying levels of narcissism as a real world extension of this work. PhD student Breanna Atkinson is the project leader.


Social Behaviour in Contexts
------

- *Microaggressions:* This project uses Experience Sampling Methods to examine how people with different personal identity characteristics report microaggressions in the context of other experiences such as positive events and non-microaggression negative events. We examine both immediate and long-term consequences of such events, including changes to mood and stress levels, as well as longer term consequences such as symptoms of depression and anxiety. This project also collects detailed information about perpetrators of microaggressions and receivers' impressions of why they occur. Negar Samani is the PhD student leading this project. 

- *Social Networks of Undergraduates:* How do incoming undergraduates build and prune their social networks? How do network strategies change the likelihood of retaining a network node or pruning it? Do interpersonal strategies such as the tendency toward co-rumination play a role in this process? The goal of this research is to answer these questions in a large-scale ego-centric study of social networks, with measurements at three time points. PhD student Samantha Jones is heading this project.


Reward and Social Cue Interpretation
------

- *A Multifaceted Study of Trust:* Social trust between individuals is the glue that holds society together. However, evidence suggests that social trust levels are generally waning ([1](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-022-09787-1), [2](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/0162-895X.00117), [3](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0049089X21000144)). Here we examine how different types of social cues including static facial physiognomy and dynamic smiles interact with fair behaviour to influence the degree to which participants trust their social partners. This project is the result of a collaboration with [Dr Danielle Shore @ Oxford University](https://www.psy.ox.ac.uk/people/danielle-shore). 

- *The Evolution of Trust:* In this project, we look at a very subtle social cue: the reliability of people's interpersonal behaviour. Using online and live versions of a 4-player public goods game, we show that different types of punishments appear to promote more reliable behaviour if the punishment and social context are matched (e.g., social punishments work very well to maintain the consistency of public contributions in face-to-face games but in the more anonymous online context, monetary punishment works better). We then ask whether manipulating the degree to which a group's contribution behaviour is reliable and fair can improve trust at the level of the group amongst individual group members. This project is a collaboration with Dr. Philippa Beston.
